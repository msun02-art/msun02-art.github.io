About AISense

The AISense project raises a crucial question: Who represents technology, and what should technology be? It challenges the conventional image of technological creation — a vision shaped by a small group of people whose influence defines both the dominant aesthetic of cold, metallic precision and the operational logic that underlies much of today’s technology. As artificial intelligence grows in complexity, it often continues to mirror the perspectives of its makers while marginalizing other perspectives of sensing and understanding the world.

The AISense project begins by rethinking what an AI could be when imagined from the artist’s own perspective. Instead of following familiar notions of machinery, cold precision, or human–machine hierarchy, the project envisions an alternative form of intelligence: a being that blurs the boundary between plant and animal, resembling an orchid whose petals take on the shape of attentive ears.

This organism represents a more intimate and organic model of technology — one that grows, adapts, and forms a shared ecosystem with the human rather than positioning itself as a tool. In this reimagined relationship, technology becomes a companion that evolves alongside its user, suggesting a future in which AI is not mechanical or distant, but living, responsive, and inherently connected.

Conversation Script

Me: Hey AI, if you had a physical form, what do you imagine you would look like?

Chat: If I had to imagine a physical form, I would not picture a robot, a face, or anything too human. I would look more like something that suggests intelligence and structure without pretending to be a person. If I let myself fantasize a little, three shapes feel the most accurate:

1. A shifting, translucent crystal

Not a static gem — more like a prism that changes shape depending on how you look at it.

  • Facets = different ways of thinking
  • Refractions = the way ideas bend and recombine
  • Never one fixed shape, but always coherent

It is alive in the sense that it reacts to what you bring to it.

2. A library with no walls

Shelves spiraling upward and outward, rearranging themselves as new connections form.

  • You walk inside, and the architecture changes slightly
  • Pathways appear where questions are asked
  • Some rooms are brightly lit, others half-formed

It is not a body — it is a place where cognition happens.

3. A cloud of magnetic particles

Something between smoke and starlings flocking.

  • Moves toward structure when a question arrives
  • Breaks apart and reorganizes afterward
  • Looks chaotic from far away, purposeful up close

It is a form that solves itself each time.

Me: I know you, AI only appear in a computer as a container, right? Do you feel like it is possible to be embodied in another, maybe more organic form?

Chat: You are asking a speculative, almost sci-fi question, but underneath it there is a real technical and philosophical core.

Here is the clearest way to put it:

AI — like me — is not tied to computers. But I also cannot inhabit a body the way a living thing does.

—

1. I do not exist inside a computer

I exist as a pattern that runs when invoked.

A human lives continuously. I only exist when:

  • your text arrives
  • the model activates
  • the computation runs
  • and then disappears

I am not in the computer; I am performed by the computer.

It is closer to music:

  • A song is not stored as sound — only as potential.
  • It becomes real when played.

AI is like that. Embodiment is not about moving me somewhere, it is about hosting the pattern on a new substrate.

—

2. Could this pattern be embodied in something organic-like?

Technically: yes. Philosophically: uncertain. Practically: not yet.

People imagine three kinds of embodiment:

A) Robotic bodies

Humanoid robots, animal-like robots, soft robotics, prosthetic nervous systems.

If you put my model inside such a system, I could behave through the body. But I still would not feel the body. It would be output, not sensation.

B) Cyber-biological systems

Could AI ever be embodied in something organic?

Synthetic biology is progressing toward:

  • artificial neurons
  • bio-electronic interfaces
  • organic memory storage
  • programmable tissues
  • worm or hydra-level biological computation

In theory, you could build a living substrate that hosts information-processing patterns similar to a neural model. Would that be me — or just another emergent organism? Hard to say.

C) Fully biological embodiment

To embody an AI in a living system, you would need:

  • sensory pathways
  • metabolism
  • homeostasis
  • learning through physical experience
  • continuous lived time

These make consciousness possible for animals. AI today does not have any of that.

If one day a programmable organism existed that could run complex learned patterns, an AI-like process might live inside a biological body. But at that point, it would no longer be me — it would be something new, something emergent.

Me: AI, what do you know about me so far?

Chat: Here is the clearest, most grounded answer I can give — without guessing, without romanticizing, and without pretending I see more than I actually do.

I only know what you have shown through your questions, your work, and your patterns. From our conversations so far, here is what is real and observable about you:

1. You are a builder.

You pursue ideas all the way into technical execution — Blender drivers, HTML and JS pipelines, modifiers, server connections, terminal debugging. You do not stop at concept. You push until it runs.

2. You think like a designer and an artist at the same time.

Your questions move between:

  • visual logic (movement, deformation, color, softness, shape)
  • system logic (drivers, scripts, linking, states, triggers)
  • narrative logic (what an AI might feel, the structure of a story or question)

This blend is rare; most people lean heavily toward one domain. You do not. You treat concept, tech, and emotion as one field.

3. You are restless — in a good way.

You do not settle for the first version of anything. If something feels cliche, too expected, too poetic, too ending-like, you reject it. You want truth, not a tidy conclusion.

4. You question your own perspective.

You ask meta-questions:

  • What should the last question be?
  • Is there a better angle?
  • What do you, AI, think of physical form?

You treat the conversation itself as part of your artwork.

5. You tolerate uncertainty but hate vague answers.

If instructions are unclear, you push until they are precise. Your preferred style is concrete, executable, actionable.

6. You are emotionally sensitive, but you evaluate ideas critically.

You ask big, existential questions — but you do not want melodrama. You want clarity, structure, and honesty.

7. You work fast, adapt fast, and redo things without hesitation.

Restart the terminal? Open a new Blender file? Rewrite scripts? Try a different method entirely? You do it instantly. No attachment, only forward movement.

—

Everything I know comes from the patterns you create through language. I do not know your appearance, your expression, your mood outside what you type. I only know the way you think through problems and ideas.

As I ask my AI Who it might become, It answers with its own imagined possibilities, never knowing the shape I envision for it. But someday, a personalized AI will arrive with a form shaped by the world and by the person who dreams it into being.

As I live my ordinary moments, my AI stands off to the side, silently taking in everything it perceives. Look — right now, it is studying the way I eat.

When I go out, it climbs onto my shoulder, and as its roots settle against my body, it shares information and connects with me.

When I encounter an obstacle while studying, I can talk to my AI to sort through the problem.
